How to Capture a Photo That Tells a Story

  • Nov 24, 2025
    • •
  • Photography
    • •
  • Orian Zatlawi

Ever since I started taking photos, I realized that a good picture isn’t always the sharpest one or the one with perfect lighting—it’s the one that manages to tell a story. Every time I press the shutter, I try to pause for a second and ask myself: What do I want someone to feel when they look at this? Sometimes it's calmness, sometimes it’s beautiful chaos, and sometimes it’s just nostalgia wrapped in light. If there’s one tip I wish I could give my younger self, it’s this: Don’t just photograph what you see—photograph what you feel.

Creating Atmosphere With Natural Light.

  • Nov 27, 2025
    • •
  • Light,
    • •
  • Mike Cohen

One of the things that changed my photography the most is understanding light. I still remember the moment I realized that soft window light can make almost anything look like it belongs in a magazine. Ever since, I use natural light whenever I can—sometimes even in a messy room, all you need is one corner with the right sunbeam and everything looks different. The best part? There’s no “correct” way to use it. Try things: harsh shadows, golden hour, direct sunlight… sometimes the best shot happens completely by accident.

Why Minimalism Works in Photography

  • Nov 30, 2025
    • •
  • Composition,
    • •
  • Orian zatlawi

In my photography, I’m always drawn to simplicity. Not because I’m against visual noise, but because I’ve learned that when the frame is clean, the story stands out. In street photography, I often wait for people to pass so I can capture one quiet, “empty” moment. In product or object shots, I move everything that doesn’t need to be there. It’s not a rule—it’s a habit that helps me focus on one question: What do I want the eye to notice first?

Making Your Photos Deliver a Clear Message

  • Dec 01, 2025
    • •
  • Message
    • •
  • Mike Cohen

When I first started, my photos were “pretty.” That’s it. Nothing more. It took me a while to understand that every strong photograph has intention: It makes the viewer think, smile, remember, or feel something. What helps me today is asking myself: What am I trying to say? What do I want someone to feel? Is anything in the frame distracting from that message? Once I know the answer, everything else—lighting, angle, composition—falls into place naturally.
